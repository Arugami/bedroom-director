## ðŸ’‹ The Double Entendre Strategy

### The Dynamic

**"Bedroom Fuel."**
- At face value â†’ sounds risquÃ©
- In context â†’ means creative stamina

**That tension is what makes it memorable.**

We don't suppress it â€” we reframe it.

### Reframing the Subtext

We make sure every visual and verbal cue pushes the audience toward:

**"Fuel for your art"**  
not  
**"Fuel for your body."**

That's what keeps it feeling like a creative rebellion, not a gimmick.

### How to Channel the Subtext Intelligently

#### **1. Tone Management**
- Keep humor + self-awareness (Ryan Reynolds style)
- Never treat it earnestly; treat it like brand irony
- Example: "Property of BD Studios / Do Not Generate Without Permission" reads as tongue-in-cheek

#### **2. Design Cues that Anchor It in Creativity**
- **Studio textures** (film grain, halftone, print bleed) = creative craft, not sensual gloss
- **Lighting** (fluorescent, amber, purple) = cinematic night, not sultry mood
- **Ribbed grip** = functional, not fetishized â€” framed like industrial design

#### **3. Copy & Voice**
Use lines that let both readings exist, but resolve into art:
- "Keep it up all night."
- "Fuel for every scene."
- "The drink that directs you."

**These all play the double game â€” clever, not crude.**

#### **4. Lore Reinforcement**
Whenever the can appears in-world (ads, posters, BD Studios props), pair it with creative context:
- Sitting beside a camera or synth
- Held by a filmmaker with tape reels
- On a cluttered desk full of storyboards

**The setting neutralizes the sexual read and reframes it as artistic stamina.**

### Position in Canon

**BEDROOM FUELâ„¢ â€“ The Original Creator Energy Drink**  
Since 1989. Created to Keep You Creating.

That tagline flips the energy: it keeps the wink but points the audience right back to creativity.
